Grigory Alexandrovich Potemkin (1739–1791) was: a military leader a statesman a political genius the most influential advisor and lover of Catherine II (Catherine the Great) and one of the most important architects of the Russian Empire’s southern expansion He was brilliant, charismatic, emotionally intense, wildly ambitious — and utterly loyal to Catherine. Potemkin was not just a court favourite. He was the de facto co-ruler of Russia for years. Their relationship blended intellect, strategy, emotion, and ambition in a way almost without parallel in European history. Where Catherine brought vision and political instinct, Potemkin brought audacity, creativity, and an obsession with expanding Russia’s reach toward the Black Sea. They shared a bond that was deeper than court politics- a relationship that survived passion, distance, rivalries, other lovers, and the brutal machinery of imperial power. Even after their official romantic affair ended, they remained inseparable as strategic partners and soul-confidants , writing intimately, advising each other constantly, and shaping the empire in tandem. Catherine trusted Potemkin with what she trusted no one else: Crimea, the southern frontier, the Black Sea Fleet, the administration of vast new territories, and foreign policy toward the Ottoman Empire. Their partnership produced decisions that still define geopolitics today. The annexation of Crimea in 1783, planned, executed, and celebrated under Potemkin’s hand- created the strategic obsession modern Russia continues to pursue. Where MDO Actually Comes From Originally, Western militaries viewed warfare through three domains : Land Air Maritime This tri-domain framework worked when: domains were largely independent, effects were slower, information moved through linear chains, technology cycles were predictable, and the battlespace was hierarchical. What quantum is (in practical security language) In simple terms: Quantum computing is a new way of computing that can solve some problems dramatically faster than today’s computers including certain problems that modern encryption relies on. Air assets could observe, strike, and return without ever physically occupying the space below. That alone changed how states thought about vulnerability, defence, and deterrence. Speed is the first defining factor. Aircraft move faster than any other military system in regular use. This means that decision windows shrink. What might take hours or days to unfold on land can develop in minutes in the air. Commanders must process information, make decisions, and execute actions under far tighter timelines. Delays are not just inefficiencies, they become operational risks. Altitude is the second. Operating above the surface provides visibility, but also exposure. Air assets can see further, but they can also be seen, tracked, and targeted across larger distances. The vertical dimension adds opportunity, but it also adds vulnerability. Control of altitude bands, radar coverage, and sensor layers becomes critical, and the fight is no longer just horizontal, but fully three-dimensional. Reach is the third. Air power extends influence across borders without the need for physical presence. It enables reconnaissance deep into adversary territory, precision strikes against distant targets, rapid reinforcement, and strategic signalling. This reach is what made air power central to deterrence. The ability to hold targets at risk from afar changes behaviour, even without direct engagement. These characteristics enabled a set of roles that became foundational. Reconnaissance transformed situational awareness. What commanders once had to guess, they could now observe. Strike capabilities allowed targets to be engaged without massing forces at the front. Mobility enabled rapid deployment of troops, supplies, and evacuation. And deterrence emerged from the visible and credible ability to project force at speed and scale. But the deeper change was not technological. It was cognitive. Air power altered how command itself works. Because of speed and reach, air operations demand centralised coordination combined with decentralised execution. Information must flow quickly across sensors, command nodes, and platforms. Decisions must be made with incomplete data and under time pressure. The traditional linear sequencing of observe, orient, decide, act becomes compressed and often overlaps. This created an expectation that air superiority could be established early and then used to shape the rest of the fight. For decades, this assumption held in many conflicts. Control of the air enabled freedom of movement, persistent surveillance, and precision strike. It allowed land and maritime forces to operate with reduced risk and greater confidence. Air power became not just a domain, but an enabler of all others. Yet even in its early history, the vulnerabilities were visible. Air assets depend on bases, maintenance, fuel, trained crews, and increasingly complex support systems. They are sensitive to weather, to electronic interference, and to disruption of navigation and communication. They are powerful, but also fragile in ways that are not always immediately apparent. What made air power dominant was not invulnerability, but the ability to operate faster, further, and more flexibly than adversaries could respond. That advantage is now under pressure. Introduction: autonomy does not remove command, it changes it Autonomy in defence is often presented as a way to reduce human burden. The narrative is familiar: machines will take over repetitive tasks, accelerate decision-making, and allow commanders to focus on higher-level intent. It sounds efficient. It sounds logical. It is also, in many cases, misleading. Because autonomy does not remove burden. It redistributes it. What changes is not whether humans are involved, but where the pressure sits, how fast decisions need to be made, and what kind of judgement is required under uncertainty . Tasks that were once sequential become parallel. Decisions that once allowed time for consultation become compressed into seconds. Situations that were previously handled through hierarchical escalation increasingly require pre-defined delegation and immediate local action. This is where the real shift begins. Autonomy forces organisations to answer questions they have often been able to postpone: What can be delegated, and at what level? Under which conditions does a system act independently, and when must it defer? What constitutes sufficient confidence in data or model output to act? Who carries responsibility when a system behaves as designed, but the outcome is still wrong? These are not technical questions. They are command questions and they are becoming unavoidable. There is also a second, less visible effect. As autonomy increases, so does cognitive load . Instead of manually executing every step, operators and commanders are now expected to monitor, validate, interpret, and intervene across multiple systems simultaneously. Alerts increase. Edge cases increase. Exceptions increase. The human role shifts from direct control to supervision under pressure, often with incomplete information and degraded communications. In other words, autonomy does not simplify command. It changes its nature. This is why the discussion cannot be reduced to “human-in-the-loop” versus “human-out-of-the-loop.” Those labels are too simplistic to capture what is actually happening in operational environments. In reality, what matters is how authority, responsibility, and decision rights are distributed across humans and machines under different conditions , including degraded ones. NATO’s approach to responsible use reflects this reality. It does not assume that autonomy replaces human judgement, but that systems must remain governable, traceable, and accountable within a human command structure . Similarly, existing autonomy policies emphasise minimising unintended outcomes and ensuring that responsibility remains clearly assigned, even when systems operate with high levels of automation. But policy alone does not solve the problem. Because once these systems enter real operational environments, they collide with everything discussed in the previous parts of this series: fragmented data, overclassification, integration barriers, inconsistent governance, and institutional risk aversion. Autonomy does not sit above those problems. It amplifies them. If the data is unreliable, autonomy scales uncertainty. If integration is weak, autonomy fragments execution. If governance is unclear, autonomy creates hesitation or overreaction. And if delegation is not properly defined, autonomy does not create speed. It creates confusion. That is why autonomy in defence is not primarily a story about machines becoming more capable. It is a story about whether organisations are able to adapt their command models, delegation logic, and institutional structures to operate under fundamentally different conditions. The real question is not how advanced the system is. It is whether the organisation using it understands what it means to delegate, when to trust, when to intervene, and how to remain accountable when the pace of action exceeds traditional command cycles . That is where autonomy becomes real. 2. When Sputnik 1 crossed the sky in 1957, it demonstrated more than technological capability. It showed that objects placed above the atmosphere could transmit information globally, predictably, and beyond the constraints that had defined warfare for centuries. This marked the beginning of a shift where observation, communication, and measurement were no longer bound to terrain, borders, or line of sight. The earliest military use of space reflected this shift. It was not about weapons, but about knowledge. Reconnaissance satellites developed in the late 1950s and early 1960s replaced slow, high-risk aerial observation with persistent, global coverage. For the first time, adversary territory could be observed continuously without physically crossing into it. Intelligence collection became more stable, more scalable, and less dependent on immediate operational risk. This changed how states understood each other’s capabilities, intentions, and movements, and reduced some forms of uncertainty while introducing new dependencies. As these capabilities expanded, they did so with a clear operational logic. Navigation became one of the most consequential developments. Systems such as Transit enabled submarines carrying nuclear payloads to determine their position with a level of precision that had not previously been possible. This was not simply a technical improvement. It directly influenced deterrence by ensuring that strategic assets could operate with confidence in their positioning and targeting. Accuracy in location translated into credibility in effect, and credibility reinforced strategic stability. This foundation evolved into the Global Positioning System, formally initiated in 1973. GPS was designed to unify fragmented navigation efforts into a single, globally accessible system capable of providing precise positioning, navigation, and timing. While it later expanded into civilian use, its origins and design logic were operational from the beginning. Timing signals became as critical as location data, enabling synchronization across systems, coordination between units, and the functioning of increasingly complex military and civilian networks. In parallel, communications moved into orbit. Early satellite systems demonstrated that signals could be relayed across continents without reliance on terrestrial infrastructure that could be disrupted or constrained by geography. This enabled command and control across theatres, real-time coordination between dispersed forces, and the ability to maintain connectivity in environments where traditional communication lines would be vulnerable or unavailable. The implications extended beyond military use, but the operational backbone remained firmly tied to defence requirements. Weather and environmental monitoring added another layer that became deeply embedded in operational planning. Satellite-based observation of atmospheric conditions allowed for more accurate forecasting of cloud cover, storm systems, and environmental factors that directly influence military activity. Air operations, sensor effectiveness, timing of manoeuvres, and logistical planning all became more informed and more predictable. This was not an auxiliary function. It became part of how operations were designed and executed. Missile warning systems introduced a further dimension that linked space directly to strategic deterrence. Satellites capable of detecting launches in near real time reduced uncertainty and compressed decision timelines at the highest levels of command. They enabled early warning, supported response planning, and contributed to the broader architecture of nuclear deterrence. In this role, space was not only supporting military operations but actively shaping the conditions under which escalation could be managed or avoided. Over time, these capabilities did not remain separate.
